Rounded corners have become the default. Open any app released in the last five years and you'll find them: gentle arcs softening every button, every card, every modal. They're supposed to feel "friendly." They've become a visual sedative.

## The Problem with "Friendly"

The rise of rounded corners tracks perfectly with the rise of "frictionless design." The philosophy goes: the smoother the visual experience, the more users will engage. Rounded corners signal *approachability*, *warmth*, *safety*.

But there's a cost. When every interface uses the same soft geometry:

- **Visual distinction collapses** — nothing stands out from anything else
- **Hierarchy disappears** — buttons look like text boxes look like panels
- **Character is erased** — the product loses any sense of identity

> "Good design is not comfortable design. It is design that communicates clearly, even if that clarity requires a little confrontation."

## The History of Sharp

It wasn't always like this. Early web interfaces were *sharp by necessity* — flat HTML rectangles with pixel borders. But as CSS evolved and gave us `border-radius`, we collectively decided: *soft is better*. We never really questioned whether that was true.

Meanwhile, in print design, newspapers and editorial layouts have been using razor-sharp grids for centuries. The *New York Times* doesn't round its columns. *The Economist* doesn't soften its masthead. **Sharp is serious. Sharp is trustworthy.**

## The Case for Corners

Sharp corners are **honest**. They don't pretend to be anything other than what they are: a rectangle. They force the designer to do the heavy lifting — to make the *content* warm, the *copy* friendly, the *interaction* delightful. The shape itself doesn't need to do that work.

Every pixel of `border-radius` is a tiny act of avoidance.

## What To Do Instead

If you want to add warmth without reaching for `border-radius`:

- Use generous white space — breathing room communicates calm
- Choose expressive typography — the right typeface does more than any soft corner
- Add color with intention — a warm background palette beats a rounded card
- Let illustrations and photography carry the personality

The corner is a *structural* element. It should be determined by the grid, not by a desire to be liked.

## Coda

None of this means rounded corners are always wrong. Sometimes they're correct — a pill-shaped tag, a circular avatar, an icon button. But they should be a *decision*, not a default. The default should be: **square**. Everything else should be justified.
